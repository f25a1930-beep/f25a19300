Interactive Integration Guide

What is a Riemann Sum?

A Riemann sum is a method for approximating the total area under a curve on a graph. It splits the interval $[a, b]$ into $n$ smaller pieces (subdivisions). On each piece, we build a simple geometric shape (like a rectangle or trapezoid) whose height is determined by evaluating the function $f(x)$ at a specific representative test point.

Supported Approximation Methods

  • Left Riemann Sum ($L_n$): Evaluates the function height at the left-hand endpoint of each subinterval ($x_i$). Usually underestimates increasing functions and overestimates decreasing ones.
  • Right Riemann Sum ($R_n$): Evaluates function height at the right-hand endpoint ($x_{i+1}$). Usually overestimates increasing functions and underestimates decreasing ones.
  • Midpoint Sum ($M_n$): Uses the precise center height of each subinterval. Highly accurate since the error cancellation of left and right segments balances out beautifully.
  • Trapezoidal Rule ($T_n$): Fits a sloped trapezoid to each interval. It evaluates both left and right endpoints and connects them with a straight line.
  • Simpson's 1/3 Rule ($S_n$): Fits a smooth quadratic parabola through consecutive triplets of points. Extremely fast convergence and high precision. Note that standard implementations require an even $n$.

Interactive Features

You can drag the dashed vertical boundaries on the graph itself to shift lower bound $a$ or upper bound $b$. Use your scroll wheel to zoom the viewport and click-drag the blank regions of the graph to pan around.
